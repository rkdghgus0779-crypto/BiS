# Fibrosis and Immune Response Study Note

Fibrosis is a biological process where tissue repair becomes excessive and leads to abnormal extracellular matrix accumulation. It can involve immune cells, fibroblasts, myofibroblasts, cytokine signaling, and tissue remodeling.

Macrophages can influence fibroblast activation through inflammatory and repair-related signals. Fibroblasts can become myofibroblasts, which produce extracellular matrix proteins and contribute to tissue stiffness.

TGF-beta signaling is often discussed as a major pathway in fibrosis because it can promote fibroblast activation and extracellular matrix production. Computational biology can help students connect these cell-level mechanisms with gene expression data and disease phenotypes.

Single-cell RNA-seq can be useful for studying fibrosis because it can separate signals from different cell populations. A beginner analysis might ask which immune cells are present, whether fibroblast states differ across samples, and which genes are associated with extracellular matrix remodeling.

This note is educational and does not provide medical advice.
